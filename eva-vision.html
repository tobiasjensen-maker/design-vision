The design question

The strategy is agentic — but what about today's product?

Our product strategy for 2026/27 is clearly centred on agentic AI: three propositions (Automation, Advisory, Platform/Partners), a skill-based architecture, and EVA as the orchestrator. But 270,000+ connected companies rely on the e-conomic they know today. The P&D question is: how do we evolve both at once?

What the strategy tells us

Agentic is the bet — move decisively

Skills become the product unit, the trust layer is a launch prerequisite, and investment shifts rapidly towards agentic from DP5 onwards. The window is open but narrowing — competitors are planning on our caution.

What this vision adds

A design path for the transition

The mass-market product doesn't stop existing when the agentic product launches. We need a deliberate P&D approach for both — different principles, different pace, different design needs — while keeping them on a shared foundation.

This vision strives to strike a balance between unlocking our agentic future and maintaining the mass-market product our firms depend on today.
Our product & design thesis

Two experiences, one shared foundation

As we execute the agentic strategy, we'll be running two product experiences in parallel — each with its own principles, pace, and design needs. But they share a foundation: the platform and trust layer, EVA's capabilities, one data model, and one brand.

These apply to every feature we ship in the mass-market product. If a design decision conflicts with one of them, it needs to be escalated — not worked around.

Trust by defaultDefaults are conservative; automation expands only as accuracy is proven in-context for that firm and that client.
Transparency of actionA bookkeeper can always see what EVA did, why, on which data, and when — and show it to a partner, a client, or an auditor in seconds.
ReversibilityAnything EVA does can be undone in one step. Irreversible actions require deliberate confirmation, never dark patterns.
Assistive, not autonomousEVA proposes and prepares; the bookkeeper decides and confirms. "Confirm" is a feature, not friction.
Familiarity preservedEVA additions are layered on the existing UI, never in place of it. A bookkeeper who used e-conomic last year feels at home next year.
Firm-shaped, not only client-shapedBulk actions, cross-client visibility, partner oversight are first-class concerns.
Progressive disclosure of AIFirms opt into deeper automation at their own pace, per client and per workflow. Safe defaults; high ceiling.
Accessibility non-negotiableReadable type, predictable layouts, keyboard and screen-reader parity, clear language. AI must not become a new accessibility barrier.

How the mass-market teams operate. Not excuses for being slow — the operating system that lets us take the right kind of risks in a regulated, trust-heavy product.

Slow is smooth, smooth is fastWe optimise for not breaking what works. The cost of a regression is measured in firm trust, support load, and churn.
Risk-managed experimentationEvery meaningful change ships behind rollout mechanics: staged exposure, firm-segment canaries, monitored success metrics, kill criteria.
Conservative prioritisationWe say no more than we say yes. A long backlog is a feature of a mature product, not a bug.
Hierarchy serves qualityDesign, engineering, compliance, and support have explicit sign-offs on customer-impacting changes.
Regulatory-grade rigorAuditability, GDPR, local tax and bookkeeping rules are non-negotiable inputs. Compliance is part of the definition of done.
Research grounded in the firmWe weight signal from the long tail of firms — small practices, sole practitioners, apprentices — not just the loudest or most sophisticated.

How we measure whether we're succeeding — across trust, adoption, efficiency, quality, and inclusivity.

  • Trust indicators: NPS among partners and bookkeepers, retention, support contact rate, CSAT on AI-assisted interactions
  • Adoption depth: % of JTBD steps where EVA suggestions are accepted; feature-level engagement per firm
  • Efficiency gained: time-per-client per JTBD (bank reconciled, supplier invoice booked, period closed)
  • Quality: correction rate on EVA suggestions, severity-weighted incident rate
  • Inclusivity: accessibility audit scores; adoption parity across firm sizes and seniority levels

Protect the core — keep the legacy product healthy with Taco as we know it today

A solid, evolving design system that lets designers and developers maintain the mass-market product at a sustainable pace, without compromising quality or retention.

  • Mature existing components into fully functional ones (e.g. Table3)
  • Deep documentation for developers and designers
  • Explicit permission for teams to build "snowflakes" where the core system doesn't yet cover their need
  • Steady investment in accessibility, tokens, and consistency

Track 2 — The agentic product

The design principles governing how the Front Door, Skills, Artefacts, and Trust Layer show up to the accounting office.

Intent over interfaceThe primary input is intent — spoken, typed, uploaded, or implied. The UI is generated in service of that intent.
Firm-scale thinking by defaultEVA thinks across the entire client portfolio. "Reconcile all clients." "Close every VAT-ready client." Firm scale is the default scope.
Trust is a feeling we design forTrust is an experience we produce deliberately every time EVA acts. Every skill ships with its trust experience, not after it.
Radical transparency of reasoningEvery action is traceable to the data, the rule, or the precedent that produced it. "Why did you do this?" is always one click away.
Trust earned through verificationEvery EVA-generated output can be checked, challenged, and audited. We invest as heavily in verification as in generation.
Agency with accountabilityAccountability lives with a named principal — the bookkeeper, the partner, the firm. Permissions and limits are first-class concepts.
Conversational is a starting pointLanguage is the opening move. From there EVA composes the right surface — a table, a chart, a decision — around the task.
Composable agents, one EVASpecialist agents cooperate behind the scenes. To the firm, it is always EVA. One voice, one memory, one identity.
Segment-adaptive experienceForward-looking firms get different defaults, autonomy ceilings, and onboarding. The Front Door adapts; the brand stays constant.
Safety by design, not frictionGuardrails are built into the agent's reasoning, not bolted on as confirmation dialogs. Safe autonomy beats safe UX theatre.

The agentic track needs a different operating system — one designed for learning velocity, not just shipping velocity.

Speed is a featureWe ship daily, measure weekly, and decide monthly. If a competitor is faster, that's a strategic failure, not a neutral fact.
Small teams, short feedback loopsFull-stack squads with design, engineering, ML, and domain expertise in the same room. No handoffs. No "phase 2."
Minimal hierarchy, maximal contextDecisions happen at the level with the most context, not the highest title. Leaders unblock; they do not gatekeep.
Experience before engineeringEvery skill is an experience promise before it is a technical one. We define the UX artefact before engineering builds.
Ship to learnEvery release is an experiment. We are as proud of things we stop doing as of things we continue. Learning velocity is the primary KPI.
Reversible decisions fastMost decisions in this track are reversible — treat them that way. Irreversible decisions get careful treatment.
Design in the open — with real firmsPrototypes over specs. Live demos over decks. A rotating cohort of partner firms is in the build loop from week one.
Higher risk tolerance — on purposeBugs are acceptable in exchange for learning. Trust-breaking incidents are not. The line between the two is a team-level craft.

How we measure learning, agent performance, trust under autonomy, and firm outcomes.

  • Learning velocity: # of experiments, % producing directional learning
  • Agent performance: task success rate, autonomous completion rate, intervention rate — per JTBD
  • Trust under autonomy: correction rate vs. confidence level, calibration accuracy, incident-free hours of autonomous operation
  • Firm outcomes: clients-per-bookkeeper, revenue-per-employee, partner NPS among opt-in firms
  • Expansion signal: % of active firms choosing EVA-as-Front-Door over legacy surfaces when both are available

What got us here won't get us there — a new foundation for the AI-native product

A new design system capability that lets teams prototype, iterate, and deliver agentic experiences at a fundamentally different speed — so our teams can out-learn the market, not just out-ship it.

  • A new package with rewritten tokens, components, and skills/md-files purpose-built for agentic UI
  • Pattern libraries for AI interface design — trust signals, review flows, orchestration surfaces, skill templates, artefact presentation
  • A move from "Design for People" to "Design for People and AI/LLMs": components that LLMs can reason about and assemble
  • A design-system-as-product mindset, with its own roadmap, consumers, and feedback loops

Timeline: Kick off the agentic design system work by the beginning of 2027, with early patterns in the hands of agentic squads from day one.

The shared foundation

Two experiences, rooted in one platform

While the two experiences diverge in surface and pace, they share the strategic foundation built in the product strategy: the platform and trust layer, EVA's orchestration and skill capabilities, one data model, and the quality financial data that forms our moat.

🏗️

Platform & trust layer

One data model, one trust layer, one permissions engine. The 4-layer architecture (Front Door, Skills, Artefacts, Trust Layer) serves both experiences.

🤖

EVA capabilities

Same EVA brain, same skill engine, same memory. Her role differs — Assistant vs. Front Door — but the capabilities compound across both.

🎓

Graduation path

Capabilities originate in the agentic experience and graduate to the mass-market once trust thresholds are met. Firms move at their own pace.

🔄

Data flywheel

Both experiences feed the same data moat. Automation generates labelled data, Advisory refines it, Platform extends it. The flywheel is shared.

The agentic experience is our R&D engine. The mass-market experience is where it scales.

Open questions & risks

What we don't know yet

Ambition requires honesty about the gaps. These are the questions we need to resolve as the vision matures — and the risks we manage against.

Key risks

Trust contagion

An agentic-track failure damages the mass-market brand. Mitigation: separate release surfaces, clear incident playbooks.

Two-culture split

Different ways of working fracture the org. Mitigation: shared EVA platform, deliberate rotation, unified craft.

Regulatory shift

Agentic AI in accounting will attract regulation. Mitigation: shape it; over-invest in auditability now.

Under-investing the core

Agentic work attracts attention; core pays the bills. Mitigation: ring-fence investment.

Design-system debt

If mass-market DS stagnates, we pay in quality and speed. Mitigation: fund both tracks of DS explicitly.

Moving too slowly

The bigger 2026 risk is caution. Mitigation: public dated commitments and a culture that rewards shipping.

Open questions worth a room

  • What's the minimum verification surface that makes autonomy feel safe?
  • Who owns the design-system-as-product function?
  • Who is the first firm we commit to taking fully agentic, and when?

Organisation & roles

The line between PM and UX is dissolving

AI changes what product work looks like. When a designer can prototype with an LLM and a PM can validate with a live mockup, the traditional handoff model becomes a bottleneck. Maintaining the old boundaries will create more confusion and disruption than removing them.

The old model

Clear lanes, clean handoffs

PM
Prioritises · Writes specs · Owns backlog
UX
Researches · Designs · Hands off to dev

Works when the problem is well-scoped, the tools are stable, and discovery is a phase — not a constant. But in an AI-native product, the problem changes shape while you're solving it.

The emerging reality

Overlapping skills, shared ownership

PM
UX
Builder

Discovery, prototyping, and delivery blur together. The person closest to the problem should be able to move it forward — regardless of whether their title says PM or UX.

Why maintaining the split hurts
🐌
Handoffs slow everything down

In an AI product, the loop from insight to prototype to validation needs to be hours, not weeks. A PM-to-UX-to-dev relay can't keep up.

🧩
Context is lost at every boundary

The person who talked to the firm should be the one sketching the solution. Every translation layer loses signal.

🤖
AI compresses the skill gap

LLMs let UXers prototype with real data and PMs generate design artefacts. The tools no longer respect our org chart.

⚡
Two tracks need versatile people

The agentic track moves fast; the core track demands depth. Both need people who can flex across discovery, design, and delivery.

The moveDon't reorganise for the sake of it. Redefine the roles so the lines match where the work actually happens.
